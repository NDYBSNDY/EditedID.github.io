Abstract

To reproduce the success of text-to-image (T2I) generation, recent works in text-to-video (T2V) generation employ large-scale text-video dataset for fine-tuning. However, such paradigm is computationally expensive. Humans have the amazing ability to learn new visual concepts from just one single exemplar. We hereby study a new T2V generation problem—One-Shot Video Generation, where only a single text-video pair is presented for training an open-domain T2V generator. Intuitively, we propose to adapt the T2I diffusion model pre-trained on massive image data for T2V generation. We make two key observations: 1) T2I models are able to generate images that align well with the verb terms; 2) extending T2I models to generate multiple images concurrently exhibits surprisingly good content consistency. To further learn continuous motion, we propose Tune-A-Video with tailored cross-frame attention and temporal attention blocks, which generates videos from text prompts via an efficient one-shot tuning of pre-trained T2I diffusion models. Tune-A-Video is capable of producing temporally-coherent videos over various applications such as change of subject or background, attribute editing, style transfer, demonstrating the versatility and effectiveness of our method.

 

Method

Our method takes as input a video and its accompanying textual description (e.g., "a man is surfing"), and leverages the text-to-image diffusion models (e.g., Stable Diffusion) for text-to-video generation. To adapt to video inputs, two modifications are introduced: 1) the spatial self-attention blocks are extended to cross-frame attention in spatiotemporal domain; 2) temporal self-attention blocks are appended. During fine-tuning, we update the query projection matrices of the cross-frame attention and cross-attention blocks, as well as the entire temporal attention blocks, using the standard diffusion training loss. During inference, we begin by inverting the input video into latent space using DDIM inversion, followed by the reverse diffusion process using the inverted latent and an edited prompt (e.g., "Spider Man is surfing on the beach, cartoon style").
